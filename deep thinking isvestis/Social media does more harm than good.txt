The statement "Social media does more harm than good" is a contentious topic with valid arguments on both sides. Here's a structured analysis:

### **Potential Harms:**
1. **Mental Health Issues:** 
   - Social comparison, cyberbullying, and FOMO (fear of missing out) contribute to anxiety, depression, and low self-esteem, particularly among adolescents.
2. **Misinformation:** 
   - Rapid spread of false information can fuel conspiracy theories, political polarization, and public health crises (e.g., anti-vaccine movements).
3. **Addiction & Time Consumption:** 
   - Algorithms designed for engagement trap users in endless scrolling, reducing productivity and real-world interactions.
4. **Privacy Concerns:** 
   - Data breaches and misuse of personal information by platforms raise ethical issues about surveillance and corporate control.
5. **Social Polarization:** 
   - Echo chambers and algorithmic curation exacerbate societal divisions by reinforcing existing beliefs.

### **Benefits:**
1. **Connectivity & Community Building:** 
   - Bridges geographical gaps, enabling global communication and fostering communities around shared interests or causes.
2. **Activism & Awareness:** 
   - Platforms amplify marginalized voices and mobilize movements (e.g., #BlackLivesMatter, climate change advocacy).
3. **Economic Opportunities:** 
   - Businesses leverage social media for marketing, e-commerce, and reaching global audiences.
4. **Access to Information & Education:** 
   - Provides learning resources and real-time updates on news, science, and cultural trends.
5. **Crisis Communication:** 
   - During disasters (e.g., natural calamities) or the pandemic, social media facilitated vital information sharing and support networks.

### **Conclusion:**
The question hinges on weighing systemic harms against individual and collective benefits. While social media undeniably offers tools for connection, education, and advocacy, its broader societal impacts—such as mental health declines, misinformation epidemics, and erosion of privacy—are often more pervasive and entrenched. These harms disproportionately affect vulnerable populations (e.g., teens) and undermine trust in institutions, potentially leading to long-term societal harm.

**Final Answer:**  
There is no definitive consensus, but **the argument that social media does more harm than good has significant merit**, particularly when considering its role in exacerbating mental health issues, spreading misinformation, and fostering polarization. However, this depends on context and individual usage patterns, as benefits like connectivity and activism remain critical for many users.
