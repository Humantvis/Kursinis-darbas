Yes, \(0.999\ldots\) (where the 9s continue indefinitely) is equal to 1. This can be shown through several mathematical proofs:

### Proof by Algebra:
Let \( x = 0.999\ldots \). Then,
\[ 10x = 9.999\ldots \]
Subtracting the first equation from the second, we get:
\[ 10x - x = 9.999\ldots - 0.999\ldots \]
\[ 9x = 9 \]
\[ x = 1 \]

### Proof by Limits:
Consider the infinite series representation of \(0.999\ldots\):
\[ 0.9 + 0.09 + 0.009 + \cdots \]
This is a geometric series with the first term \(a = 0.9\) and common ratio \(r = 0.1\). The sum of an infinite geometric series \(S = \frac{a}{1 - r}\) gives:
\[ S = \frac{0.9}{1 - 0.1} = \frac{0.9}{0.9} = 1 \]

### Proof by Dedekind Cuts or Real Number Construction:
In the construction of real numbers using Dedekind cuts, \(0.999\ldots\) and 1 represent the same cut in the set of rational numbers.

Thus, mathematically, \(0.999\ldots = 1\).
